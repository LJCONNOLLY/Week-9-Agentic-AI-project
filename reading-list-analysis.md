# Thematic Analysis: Core Ph.D. Candidacy Reading List 2025–2027

This document analyzes how the 55 texts on the reading list connect to each other thematically, theoretically, and intellectually.

---

## Overview

This reading list sits at the intersection of four large disciplinary formations: **digital humanities**, **critical race and feminist theory**, **media and technology studies**, and **rhetoric/technical communication**. Spanning roughly 1936–2024, it reveals a coherent scholarly project concerned above all with **power as it operates through information systems, archives, interfaces, and designed objects** — and with the communities who resist, reroute, or reimagine those systems.

---

## Cluster 1: Theoretical Foundations of Media, Meaning, and Representation

**Core texts:** Barthes (3), Benjamin W. (6), Hall (22), Baudrillard (4), Ong (44), Foucault (18), Said (49), Kuhn (28)

These are the epistemological bedrock of the list — the texts that established the intellectual grammar (sign, simulation, discourse, paradigm, encoding) that virtually every other text either inherits, critiques, extends, or deconstructs.

- **Walter Benjamin** (6) and **Roland Barthes** (3) constitute the foundational dual inheritance of media semiotics and cultural theory. Benjamin's argument about mechanical reproduction echoes throughout Brock (9), De Kosnik (15), and Manovich (33) in debates about what digital reproduction does to cultural objects and community memory. Barthes' image analysis feeds directly into Gillian Rose (47) and Nakamura (42).
- **Stuart Hall** (22) is the pivot to the critical race/feminist digital studies cluster. His encoding/decoding model — that audiences actively decode media messages in ways that may resist, negotiate, or accept dominant ideologies — is the direct theoretical ancestor of Bailey (2), Steele (50), and Gray (21).
- **Baudrillard** (4) creates productive friction with the empirical and activist texts: if simulation has replaced the real, how can we speak of the real harms of algorithmic discrimination (Noble, Eubanks)? The list stages this tension rather than resolving it.
- **Foucault** (18) contributes the concept of episteme — underlying grids of intelligibility — feeding into Kuhn (28) on paradigms and the critiques of "objectivity" in D'Ignazio & Klein (14) and Noble (43). Notably, Brooke Foucault Welles co-authors #HashtagActivism (26), signaling awareness of this inheritance.
- **Said** (49) introduces postcolonial critique — that representation is entangled with power — running directly into Risam (46), Miron (37), and Martinez (34).
- **Ong** (44) on orality/literacy connects backward to Benjamin and forward to Hayles (24), Vee (53), and Bolter (8).

## Cluster 2: Media, Technology, and Cultural Form

**Core texts:** Hayles (24), Haraway (23), Bolter (8), Manovich (33), Chun (10), Wardrip-Fruin & Montfort (55), Bogost (7), Vee (53)

- **Haraway** (23) is among the most cited thinkers across the list. Her Cyborg Manifesto refuses the nature/culture, human/machine divide and is foundational for Hayles (24), Chun (10), and the feminist design/data cluster.
- **Hayles** (24) argues that information became conceptually "disembodied" in cybernetics, with political consequences for gender, cognition, and power. This links directly to Losh & Wernimont (32), D'Ignazio & Klein (14), and the critical algorithm cluster. The question she poses — *what gets left out when we abstract to information?* — is the master question of the list.
- **Bolter** (8) contributes the concept of *remediation* (new media absorb and refashion old media), connecting Ong (44) to Vee (53) and Wardrip-Fruin & Montfort (55).
- **Manovich** (33) represents cultural analytics — computational methods applied to culture at scale — connecting to Cohen & Rosenzweig (11) and Crymble (13), and in tension with D'Ignazio & Klein (14) on whose culture gets measured.
- **Chun** (10) on "habitual new media" connects Baudrillard's simulation to Brock (9) on how Black users navigate digital infrastructure.
- **Bogost** (7) on procedural rhetoric (games make arguments through rules, not just narratives) connects to Gray (21) on how game spaces encode racial hierarchies, and to Vee (53) on programming as writing.

## Cluster 3: Critical Race, Feminist, and Queer Digital Studies

**Core texts:** Noble (43), Benjamin R. (5), Eubanks (17), Brock (9), Nakamura (42), Bailey (2), Steele (50, 51), Gray (21), Jackson, Bailey & Welles (26), McKinney (35), Losh & Wernimont (32), D'Ignazio & Klein (14), Costanza-Chock (12)

This is the intellectual center of gravity of the list — constituting what might be called the **critical infrastructure studies** turn in digital humanities.

- **Noble** (43) and **Eubanks** (17) belong together: Noble shows how search algorithms encode anti-Black racism; Eubanks shows how automated decision systems harm the poor. Together they provide the empirical foundation for **Ruha Benjamin** (5), whose "New Jim Code" is the most theoretically ambitious synthesis: technology actively functions as a tool of racial governance, and *abolition*, not reform, is required.
- **Brock** (9) provides a different analytic — rather than focusing on harm, he develops Critical Technocultural Discourse Analysis (CTDA) to study Black Americans building culture through and despite digital infrastructure. Drawing on Hall (22) and Chun (10), he insists on Black digital abundance, not just victimization.
- **Bailey** (2) introduces *misogynoir* (anti-Black misogyny in digital spaces). She appears twice — also as co-author of #HashtagActivism (26) — tracking from specific oppressions to collective political power.
- **Steele** (50, 51) also appears twice, moving from theoretical statement (*what is Black digital feminism?*) to methodological manifesto (*how do we practice Black digital humanities with radical intentionality?*).
- **Nakamura** (42), one of the earliest scholars of race and the internet, provides the foundational visual culture analysis others build on.
- **Gray** (21) extends this into gaming, taking Bogost's (7) procedural rhetoric and asking what happens when the procedures are racist. She connects to Bailey (2) and Steele (50) through Black feminist method.
- **Costanza-Chock** (12) on Design Justice pivots between this cluster and the technical communication cluster, connecting feminist theory to design practice.
- **McKinney** (35) extends the critical infrastructure lens to queer history — tracing how lesbian communities built media technologies outside hostile mainstream infrastructure — connecting to De Kosnik (15) on rogue archives.

## Cluster 4: Archives, Memory, and Difficult History

**Core texts:** De Kosnik (15), Miron (37), Lehrer et al. (30), Rose & Holloway (48), Cohen & Rosenzweig (11), Crymble (13), Milligan (36), Headrick (25), Misa (38)

- **De Kosnik** (15) on "rogue archives" — unofficial repositories outside institutional control — connects to McKinney (35), Miron (37), and Bailey (2) on community-controlled cultural preservation.
- **Miron** (37) argues that Indigenous-controlled digital archiving is a form of sovereignty and anti-colonial practice, connecting to Risam (46) and Said (49).
- **Lehrer et al.** (30) and **Rose & Holloway** (48) address museum representation of violent and contested histories, connecting to Martinez (34) on counterstory and to the DH texts on digital tools changing the ethics of memory work.
- **Cohen & Rosenzweig** (11), **Crymble** (13), and **Milligan** (36) form the digital history sub-cluster, all concerned with how digital abundance changes historical methodology — connecting to Manovich (33) on scale and D'Ignazio & Klein (14) on the critique of scale.
- **Headrick** (25) and **Misa** (38) provide the longue durée: history of information systems from pre-modernity to the present, a "slow scholarship" counterpoint to the urgency of the critical tech cluster.

## Cluster 5: Rhetoric, Technical Communication, and Design Justice

**Core texts:** Walton, Moore & Jones (54), Gonzales (20), Tham (52), Mueller (40), Martinez (34), Vee (53), Raign (45), Costanza-Chock (12)

- **Walton, Moore & Jones** (54) argues that technical communication must undergo a "social justice turn," moving from clarity/efficiency to centering equity and access. This connects to Costanza-Chock (12) and D'Ignazio & Klein (14) — the same argument in three disciplinary registers.
- **Gonzales** (20) on multilingual experiences extends this into language diversity, connecting to postcolonial DH (Risam, Said) and Martinez (34) — language is never neutral.
- **Mueller** (40) on network sense uses visualization to study rhetoric as a discipline — a reflexive move connecting him to Drucker (16) and Gold & Klein (19).
- **Vee** (53) on coding literacy is the hinge between this cluster and DH: programming as writing, connected to Bolter (8), Montfort (39), and Bogost (7), while raising equity questions connecting back to Walton et al. (54) and Eubanks (17).
- **Raign** (45) grounds professional writing in long historical perspective, connecting to Headrick (25) and Misa (38).

## Cluster 6: Digital Humanities as a Field (Meta-Texts)

**Core texts:** Gold & Klein (19), Klein/Thompson (27), Drucker (16), Losh & Wernimont (32), Risam (46), Mullaney et al. (41)

- **Gold & Klein** (19) — the *Debates in the Digital Humanities* series is the most important ongoing meta-conversation in the field. Lauren F. Klein's double presence here and in Data Feminism (14) makes her a key connective node.
- **Risam** (46) argues for a postcolonial DH: the field's infrastructure was built with Western/colonial assumptions and must be actively decolonized. Connects to Said (49), Miron (37), McKinney (35), and Mullaney et al. (41).
- **Mullaney et al.** (41) — *Your Computer Is on Fire* — argues that computing infrastructure carries the history of choices made to build it, which encoded specific power relations. Connects to Benjamin R. (5), Noble (43), and Costanza-Chock (12).
- **Drucker** (16) is more formalist and less politically urgent than the others, emphasizing humanistic method and critical analysis of digital tools — a productive counterpoint.

## Cross-Cutting Genealogies

**Genealogy 1: From Hall to Black Digital Studies**
Hall (22) → Nakamura (42) → Brock (9) → Bailey (2) → Steele (50, 51)
Each step builds on the previous: contested meaning at reception → race and the internet → Black cultural methodology → misogynoir → Black digital feminism as practice.

**Genealogy 2: From Foucault/Said to Decolonial DH**
Foucault (18) → Said (49) → Haraway (23) → Hayles (24) → Risam (46) → Miron (37)
Poststructuralist power/knowledge → postcolonial representation → feminist science studies → postcolonial digital humanities.

**Genealogy 3: From Semiotics to Data Critique**
Barthes (3) → Hall (22) → Baudrillard (4) → Hayles (24) → Manovich (33) → D'Ignazio & Klein (14) → Noble (43)
As "data" becomes the dominant sign form, how it encodes power becomes the central analytical problem.

**Genealogy 4: From Orality/Literacy to Coding Literacy**
Ong (44) → Bolter (8) → Vee (53) → Montfort (39) → Bogost (7)
Communication technologies restructure thought → digital writing → code as literacy → writing in/through systems.

**Genealogy 5: The Justice Turn in Design and Technical Communication**
Haraway (23) → D'Ignazio & Klein (14) → Costanza-Chock (12) → Walton et al. (54) → Gonzales (20) → Tham (52)
Feminist theory from critical thought into design and professional practice.

## Key Theoretical Tensions

| Tension | One Side | Other Side |
|---|---|---|
| **Critique vs. Construction** | Noble, Eubanks, R. Benjamin (abolition) | Costanza-Chock, Walton et al. (redesign from within) |
| **Representation vs. Infrastructure** | Hall, Nakamura, Barthes | Noble, Eubanks, Chun, Mullaney et al. |
| **Quantitative Scale vs. Feminist Particularity** | Manovich, Crymble, Milligan | D'Ignazio & Klein, Drucker |
| **Technological Determinism vs. Social Construction** | Ong, Hayles, Chun | Latour (ANT), most critical race texts |
| **Digital Optimism vs. Digital Harm** | Cohen & Rosenzweig, Headrick, De Kosnik | Noble, Eubanks, Benjamin R. |

## The List as an Intellectual Argument

Read as a whole, this reading list makes a coherent argument: **digital technologies do not exist outside of history, power, and embodiment, and the work of digital humanities must be accountable to the communities most affected by the systems it studies and builds.**

The list begins with foundational media theorists (Barthes, Benjamin W., Hall, Foucault) who established that meaning, knowledge, and representation are always political. It moves through postcolonial and feminist extensions (Said, Haraway, Hayles), grounds those theoretical moves in empirical cases of algorithmic harm (Noble, Eubanks, Benjamin R.) and in studies of Black, Indigenous, queer, and feminist digital practice as resistance (Brock, Bailey, Steele, McKinney, Miron). It culminates in questions of method and practice: how do we do digital humanities, technical communication, and design in ways that are accountable to justice (D'Ignazio & Klein, Costanza-Chock, Walton et al., Risam)?

The presence of Kuhn (28) and Latour (29) suggests the list is also asking a meta-question: are we in the middle of a paradigm shift in how we understand the relationship between technology and human life? The weight of evidence across these 55 texts suggests the answer is yes — and that the new paradigm will be one in which **equity, embodiment, and power are not add-ons to digital humanities but its constitutive concerns**.
